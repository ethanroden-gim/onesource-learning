The Three Core Systems

Operational record — everything about the customer, property, and service

  • Customer and property records
  • Service addresses and locations
  • Scheduling and appointment updates
  • Technician-relevant notes
  • Service history
  • Estimates, agreements, invoices

All inbound and outbound phone communication

  • Answering incoming calls
  • Returning missed calls
  • Checking voicemails
  • Outbound scheduling calls
  • Follow-up call handling

Shared inboxes, email threads, and team coordination

  • Customer email threads
  • Shared inbox management
  • Thread assignments
  • Internal comments to teammates
  • Team follow-up tracking
Core Rule Use the right system for the right task — and always document the next step so nothing is left to memory.
In Practice

You will use three primary systems. FieldRoutes is the operational source for customer records, scheduling, service history, and account details. Google Voice handles calls and voicemails. Front is where shared inbox communication and internal coordination happen.

A key part of your job is knowing where information belongs so nothing gets lost between systems or left undocumented.

Section 2 of 5

FieldRoutes: Operational Record

Use FieldRoutes for:
  • Customer and property records
  • Service addresses
  • Contact details
  • Scheduling and appointment updates
  • Technician-relevant notes (access, pets, restrictions)
  • Service history
  • Estimates or service agreement support
  • Invoice and payment visibility
Separate Practice Activity
You will practice entering and locating records directly in FieldRoutes as a hands-on exercise — see your trainer for instructions.
Documentation Standard

If it affects service, scheduling, billing, access, or technician instructions — it belongs in FieldRoutes, entered at the time of the conversation.

In Practice

Think of FieldRoutes as the operational record. If a technician needs the information to complete service, or if the office needs it for scheduling, billing, or account history, it should be captured here — not in notes, not in your head, and not in a text message.

Examples: access notes, appointment changes, pest issue details, service location, customer contact updates, and account authorization status. A technician arriving on-site will rely on what you entered. Missing details create problems in the field.

📋
Documentation Rule
Enter notes at the time of the conversation — not later. Information held in memory gets lost, changed, or forgotten before the next person needs it.
Section 3 of 5

Google Voice: Phone Handling

Use Google Voice for:
📲
Incoming Calls
Answer and handle all inbound customer calls professionally
↩️
Return Missed Calls
Follow up on any calls that weren't answered promptly
🎙️
Check Voicemails
Review, transcribe key details, and document
📤
Outbound Calls
Scheduling confirmations, follow-ups, and callbacks
Never rely on memory for:
Access details · Customer concerns · Complaints · Promised follow-up · Scheduling commitments

After every call — in this order:

1
Summarize the issue
Write down who called, what property, what concern, and relevant details from the conversation
2
Update the correct system
Operational details → FieldRoutes. Emails/threads → Front. Do not leave information only in your head.
3
Assign or escalate if needed
If someone else owns the next step, assign it clearly in the correct system. Don't assume it will be picked up.
4
Confirm the next step is clear
The call is complete only when details are documented and the next action is defined — not when you hang up.
📝
Documentation Rule
Phone conversations move fast. A call that ends without a written summary is a call that creates follow-up confusion. Document while the details are fresh.
In Practice

A good call is not complete just because you answered politely. It is complete when the relevant details are captured, the right next step is clear, and the right person or system has been updated.

Front: Shared Inbox & Team Communication

Use Front for:
📧
Customer Email Threads
All inbound and outbound customer email
📥
Shared Inbox Management
Monitor, triage, and action incoming messages
👤
Thread Assignments
Assign ownership so one person is clearly responsible
💬
Internal Comments
Communicate with teammates without emailing the customer
🔄
Team Follow-up
Track open items so nothing is dropped

Best Practices — tap to expand:

📖
Read the full thread before replying
Check what was already said, promised, or decided. Replying without reading creates duplicate work and contradictions that damage trust.
💬
Use internal comments for team notes
If you need to ask a teammate a question or add context, use an internal comment — not a reply that goes to the customer.
✋
Assign ownership when needed
If someone else owns the next step, assign the thread to them. An unassigned thread is a thread at risk of being missed.
✅
Never leave unclear next steps
Before closing or leaving a thread, confirm the next action is documented. Ambiguity is how promises get broken.
📋
Documentation Rule
Anyone reviewing a thread should be able to tell what happened, what was promised, and what needs to happen next — without asking anyone.
In Practice
Before replying, read the full thread so you do not duplicate work or miss context. Assign threads when someone else owns the next step. Use internal comments to communicate internally. The goal is a thread that tells the full story.
Section 5 of 5

Documentation Examples

Good documentation is specific. It tells the team who contacted us, where the issue is, what they experienced, when it started, any access or scheduling constraints, and what needs to happen next. A weak note creates more work. A strong note lets the next person act confidently.

WeakIncomplete — creates more questions
"Customer has mice. Wants service."
No name, contact, or property
No unit number or address
No detail on what was observed or when
No access, pet, or scheduling notes
No next step or authorization status
StrongComplete — the next person can act
"6/4: Jane Smith, resident at Maple Court, Bldg B Unit 204, reports scratching sounds in kitchen wall for 3 nights and droppings under sink. Has not notified property manager. No pets. Available weekdays after 3 PM. Needs account authorization checked before scheduling."
Date, name, and location recorded
Specific observations and timeline
Access and scheduling constraints noted
Authorization status flagged as next step
Every strong note includes these elements:
Date & time Who called Property & unit What they reported When it started Access & pets Availability Authorization status Clear next step
In Practice
The difference between these two notes is the difference between a technician who arrives prepared and one who has to call the office from the driveway. Every field in the strong note exists because someone, at some point, needed that information and didn't have it. Build the habit of capturing it the first time.
