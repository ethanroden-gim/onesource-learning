Learning Objective

You will be able to classify customer issues by urgency and determine whether to resolve, document, schedule, or escalate — and document escalations completely.

Judgment Rule Part of your job is reading the urgency of a situation accurately. When in doubt, treat it as one tier higher and escalate. The cost of over-escalating is low. The cost of under-escalating can be significant.
In Practice
Some requests are routine — confirming an appointment or sending an invoice copy. Others need closer review, especially if they involve multiple units, repeated service concerns, or product safety questions. Anything involving exposure, illness, legal concerns, or safety risks must be escalated immediately according to company process.
Section 2 of 3

Escalation Triggers

Escalate when any of these are present. You do not need all of them — one is enough.

⚕️
Safety & Health
Product-specific safety questions, health concerns, pregnancy, asthma, allergy, or any report of exposure or illness after service
⚠️
Service Concern
Repeated callbacks for the same issue, unresolved problems, possible termite or structural concern, bed bugs in a multi-unit property
🏢
Relationship & Authority
Angry board member or property manager, escalating tone, threatened complaint or review, senior stakeholder involved
🔧
Operational
Technician access failure affecting service quality, wildlife safety risk, any situation that could affect BuzzKill's liability or reputation
📋
Always Escalate
Legal, insurance, or liability language in any communication. Any situation you are unsure how to handle. "I don't know" is the start of doing the right thing — escalate and document.
Escalation Is Not Failure
When a question goes beyond your authority or knowledge, route it. It is always better to say "I'm going to route this to the right person" than to guess and create a larger problem. Escalation is part of doing the job well.
In Practice
Escalation is especially important for safety, health, liability, and sensitive customer relationships. The person you escalate to needs to know about the issue while there is still time to act on it properly.
Section 3 of 3

Documentation for Escalation

Escalation Note — Required Fields
1
Date and time
Exact time of contact or incident
2
Who contacted us
Name, role, and contact method
3
Property, community, or address
Full location including unit or common area
4
What was reported
Exactly what the customer said — not your interpretation
5
What the customer requested
Specific ask or outcome they want
6
What was promised
Anything you said would happen — be exact
7
Who was notified
Manager, technician, or team member alerted
8
Recommended next step
What you believe should happen and by when
Vague Escalation
"Customer called and is upset about mice. Escalating."
Strong Escalation
"6/4 10:15am — Jane Smith, resident, Maple Court Bldg B Unit 204, called to report continued scratching sounds and droppings after last week's treatment. Customer is requesting a same-day callback and is considering contacting the board. No promises made. Notified manager. Recommend priority callback review."
Be Factual — Not Editorial
Document what the customer said, not what you think they meant or whether you agree with their concern. The person reviewing the escalation needs facts, not interpretation. Stick to specifics.
In Practice
When you escalate, give the next person enough detail to act. A vague escalation creates more back-and-forth and delays resolution. A strong escalation note lets the manager or technician act immediately with full context.
