Required Greeting — use this every time

Warm

🔊
Clear
Spoken slowly enough to understand
🧘
Not Rushed
No matter how busy the queue is
🤝
Helpful

📋
Process-Ready

In Practice

The greeting matters because it creates consistency and professionalism. Customers may be anxious, frustrated, or embarrassed about their pest situation. A calm, organized greeting lowers the temperature immediately and signals that BuzzKill is ready to help — not reactive or disorganized.

Use this greeting on every incoming call, without exception. It is not optional, and it is not something to skip when you are busy.

Section 2 of 6

Alternative Openers

Returning a Voicemail
"Thank you for choosing BuzzKill Pest Control, this is [Name]. I'm returning your call about [topic]."
Name the reason for the call immediately so the customer knows why you're reaching out.
Scheduling Call
"Thank you for choosing BuzzKill Pest Control, this is [Name]. I'm calling to help coordinate your upcoming service."
Explain that you are coordinating service — this frames the call as helpful and expected.
Calling a Property Manager
"Thank you for choosing BuzzKill Pest Control, this is [Name]. I'm reaching out regarding service at [property/community name]."
Reference the property or community name so they immediately have the context they need.
📋
Consistency Rule
The BuzzKill greeting stays intact in all three openers. You adapt the middle — the reason for the call — but never drop the opening or your name. Every caller should know immediately who they are speaking with and that they reached BuzzKill.
In Practice
When returning a voicemail, name the reason for the call. When scheduling, explain that you are coordinating service. When calling a property manager, reference the property name so they have immediate context — property managers handle multiple accounts and need to orient quickly.
Section 3 of 6

Communication Tone

BuzzKill communication is:
Calm — even when the customer isn't
Clear — specific, not vague
Respectful — always, regardless of tone received
Practical — focused on the next step
Accurate — no guessing, no promises not backed up
BuzzKill communication is never:
Defensive — no justifying or blame-shifting
Dismissive — customers' concerns are always valid
Overly technical — avoid jargon the customer won't understand
Rushed — take the time to get details right
Imprecise — vague answers create more calls
Remember
Customers don't just need an answer. They need confidence that the issue is being handled. Your tone is part of what delivers that confidence — even when you don't have the final answer yet.
In Practice

Pest issues can feel personal and urgent. A customer may be worried about health, property damage, embarrassment, pets, children, or tenant complaints. You do not need to have every answer immediately. You do need to show that you are taking the concern seriously and moving it to the right next step.

Section 4 of 6

Suggested Responses for Common Situations

Worried Customer
Customer is anxious, stressed, or upset about a pest concern
"I understand why that would be concerning. Let me collect a few details so we can route this properly."
Technical Question
Customer asks about treatment methods, product types, or pest identification
"I don't want to guess or give inaccurate information. I'll document that and have the technician or manager review it."
Safety Question
Customer asks about product safety for people, pets, or children
"For product-specific safety information, we refer to the SDS and product label. I can help route that question to the right person."
Urgent Request
Customer needs service quickly or is pressuring for a same-day response
"I'll check the schedule and make sure we capture the right details so the technician is prepared."
The pattern behind every strong response:
Acknowledge → Collect Details → Document → Route
In Practice
These responses acknowledge the customer without overpromising. For safety questions, refer to the SDS and product label rather than answering from memory. For technical questions, involve the technician or manager. Stay within your role — that protects the customer and BuzzKill.

Handling Upset Customers

When a customer is upset:
1
Let them explain
2
Acknowledge the concern
3
Avoid arguing
4
Ask clarifying questions
5
Check service history
6
Document the facts
7
Escalate when appropriate
Suggested Response
I understand this is frustrating. I'm going to document the details, check the service history, and make sure the right person is looped in.
Never do this:
Blame the customer, technician, property manager, or board
Take frustration personally or become defensive
Make promises you cannot keep to de-escalate
Dismiss the concern or minimize the problem
In Practice
Customers may be upset because they are still seeing activity, expected a different outcome, or feel the issue is urgent. Your job is to slow the conversation down, collect facts, and avoid defensiveness. Use neutral language and document what was reported — not your interpretation of it.
Section 6 of 6

Written Communication Standards

Good written responses are:
✏️ Clear — no ambiguous language
⚖️ Brief but complete — covers what matters
🏢 Professional — appropriate for any reader
➡️ Specific about next steps — never vague
🚫 Free of unsupported promises
💬
Internal vs. Customer-Facing
Use internal comments in Front for teammate questions. Never put internal notes in a customer-facing reply. If you need to ask your team something, comment internally first.
Sample Association Email Response
Strong written response:
Thank you for reaching out. I've documented the reported activity in Building 2, Units 104 and 207, and the common laundry area. I'm going to route this for review and scheduling guidance. We'll follow up with the next available service options.
In Practice
Confirm what you understood. State what you are doing next. For association work, include property, building, unit, and common area details when relevant. Avoid making unsupported promises. Keep it professional enough that a board member, property manager, or technician could all read it and understand exactly what is happening.
