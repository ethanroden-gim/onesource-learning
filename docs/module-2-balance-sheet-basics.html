Module 2

Balance Sheet basics

Learning Objective

By the end of this module, you will be able to explain the purpose of the Balance Sheet, identify assets, liabilities, and equity, and recognize which Balance Sheet questions should be escalated to finance.

What you will learn

Module focus

  • What a Balance Sheet is used for
  • How assets, liabilities, and equity work together
  • How to explain cash, receivables, payables, and loans
  • Where property managers should avoid accounting interpretation
GIM standard

Explain the position clearly

The Balance Sheet is often where board members look for cash, debt, and financial position. Keep explanations factual, report-based, and easy to follow.

Key idea

The Balance Sheet answers: “Where does the association stand financially as of the report date?”

Purpose

A point-in-time view of financial position

Unlike an Income Statement, which shows activity over a period, the Balance Sheet shows balances as of one specific date.

The Balance Sheet helps answer:

  • What does the association have?
  • What is owed to the association?
  • What does the association owe?
  • What is the overall equity position?

Sample report context

  • Association: Test Association
  • Report: Balance Sheet
  • Date: As of 4/30/2026
  • Basis: Accrual Basis

Buildium tip

Use “as of” language when explaining this report. A Balance Sheet does not explain every transaction that happened during the month; it shows the ending position as of the date shown.

Structure

Assets, liabilities, and equity

A Balance Sheet is organized around three core sections. Property managers should be able to define each section in plain language.

Assets

What the association has, plus amounts owed to the association. Examples include bank balances, receivables, and funds in transit.

Liabilities

What the association owes to others. Examples include unpaid bills, loans, and other obligations.

Equity

The association’s accumulated financial position. Equity is affected by prior activity and current net income or loss.

Basic formula

Assets = Liabilities + Equity. The Balance Sheet must stay in balance.

Simple board-facing explanation

“This report shows what the association has, what it owes, and the resulting equity position as of the report date.”

Assets

What the association has or is owed

The sample Balance Sheet shows total current assets of $6,630. These are the resources reflected in Buildium as of 4/30/2026.

Accounts Receivable $1,430 — amounts owed to the association, such as owner charges that have been billed but not yet paid.
Funds in Transit $200 — money in process that may not yet be fully settled into the bank account.
Test Bank Account $5,000 — the cash balance reflected in Buildium for the listed bank account.
Total Current Assets $6,630 — the total of the current asset lines shown on the report.

How to say it

“As of April 30, Buildium shows total assets of $6,630, made up of receivables, funds in transit, and the bank account balance.”

Liabilities

What the association owes

Liabilities show obligations of the association. In the sample, Buildium shows both a current liability and a long-term liability.

Accounts Payable $1,000 — amounts shown as owed to vendors or other parties as of the report date.
Total Current Liability $1,000 — the total short-term liability shown in the sample report.
Loan Payable $10,000 — a debt obligation shown under long-term liabilities.
Total Liabilities $11,000 — the combined total of current and long-term liabilities.

Watch the boundary

A PM can explain that the report shows a payable or loan balance. Finance should confirm classification, loan terms, reconciliation, or whether a balance should still be open.

Equity

Equity is where PMs should be careful

Equity reflects the association’s accumulated financial position. It can be affected by historical activity, opening balances, retained earnings, and current net income.

Opening Balance Equity ($8,300) — an opening or setup-related equity balance. Questions about why it is negative should go to finance.
Retained Earnings $0 — accumulated prior earnings shown in the equity section.
Net Income $3,930 — current activity that affects the association’s equity position.
Total Equity ($4,370) — total equity shown after combining the equity lines.

Use caution

PMs may explain that equity is the difference between assets and liabilities. Do not guess why equity is negative, why an opening balance exists, or whether it should be adjusted.

Board-facing explanation

Use the Balance Sheet to answer position questions

When a board member asks about cash, receivables, bills, or debt, the Balance Sheet is usually the starting point.

PM can usually answer

Cash: bank account line Owed to association: accounts receivable Unpaid bills: accounts payable Debt: loan payable Report date: “as of” date

Escalate to finance

Why is equity negative? Was this opening balance correct? Should this payable still be open? Is the loan classified correctly? Does the balance need adjustment?

Recommended phrasing

“The Balance Sheet shows the association’s position as of April 30. I can explain what Buildium is showing here, and finance can confirm the accounting detail behind the balance.”

Wrap-up

Balance Sheet key takeaways

Module key takeaways

  • The Balance Sheet shows financial position as of a specific date.
  • Assets are what the association has or is owed.
  • Liabilities are what the association owes.
  • Equity reflects the association’s accumulated financial position.
  • Accounting setup, classification, reconciliation, and correction questions should be escalated to finance.

PM standard

Start with what the Buildium report is showing. Use the report date, line item, and amount. Avoid unsupported accounting conclusions.
